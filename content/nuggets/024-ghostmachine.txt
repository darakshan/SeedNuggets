TBD — Gilbert Ryle coined "the ghost in the machine" in 1949 to mock Cartesian dualism: the idea that somewhere inside the body's physical mechanism there must be a separate mental thing doing the real work. Ryle's point was that this is a category mistake — you can't ask where the mind is in the brain the way you ask where the engine is in the car. They are different kinds of things. But nugget #21 notes that Ryle's correction runs the risk of its own error in the other direction: dismissing the ghost entirely rather than just relocating it. If mind is not a separate substance haunting the machine, what is it? Whitehead's answer — that experience goes all the way down, that the ghost is not separate from the machine but is what the machine is like from the inside — is the thread connecting this nugget to #2, #3, and #16.
